Mummies Section

Mummy of King Seqenenre Taa II

Seqenenre Tao was a pharaoh of the 17th Dynasty who began the fight against the Hyksos. He played a key role in reviving Egyptian national identity during foreign rule. His violent death in battle made him a symbol of resistance and bravery.

Mummy of king Amenhotep II

Amenhotep II was the seventh pharaoh of Egypt’s 18th Dynasty, ruling around 1427–1401 BCE. He was known for his athletic strength and led successful military campaigns to maintain Egypt’s empire. He continued his father Thutmose III’s expansionist policies and built temples, especially in Karnak.

Mummy of king Ahmose I

Ahmose I was the founder of Egypt’s 18th Dynasty and ruled around 1550–1525 BCE. He successfully expelled the Hyksos from Egypt, ending the Second Intermediate Period. Ahmose rebuilt Egypt’s economy and army, and launched campaigns into Nubia and the Levant. His reign marked the beginning of the New Kingdom, Egypt’s most powerful era.

Mummy of King Ramses II

Ramses II was the third pharaoh of the 19th Dynasty, ruling for 66 years. He led major battles, including the famous Battle of Kadesh. He signed the first recorded peace treaty in history with the Hittites. Ramses II built great temples like Abu Simbel and is known as Ramses the Great.

Mummy of King Seti I

Seti I was the second pharaoh of Egypt’s 19th Dynasty, ruling from 1290–1279 BCE. He conducted military campaigns in Syria and Canaan, strengthening Egypt’s empire. Seti I built magnificent temples, particularly at Abydos and Karnak. His tomb in the Valley of the Kings is one of the most beautifully decorated.

Mummy of King Thutmose III

Thutmose III was the sixth pharaoh of the 18th Dynasty and ruled for over 50 years. He was a brilliant military leader, often called the "Napoleon of ancient Egypt." He expanded Egypt’s empire to its greatest extent through successful campaigns in Asia and Nubia. Thutmose III also promoted art, architecture, and temple building, especially at Karnak.

Mummy of Queen Hatshepsut

The “Foremost of Noble Ladies,” Hatshepsut’s achievements as a powerful queen and then a ruling pharaoh have made her one of the most famous figures in ancient Egypt history. She was the daughter of Thutmose I and his principal queen Ahmose, who may have been part of the Ahmosid dynasty that founded the New Kingdom.

Mummy of King Merenptah

Merenptah was the thirteenth son of Ramses II and his second Great Royal Wife. Due to his father’s very long reign, he ascended the throne at a more advanced age, and ruled for only about 11 years. He was an active king, engaging in a number of military campaigns: he held off a coalition of the Libyans and Sea Peoples that attacked Egypt’s Delta in his fifth regnal year; and put down a rebellion in Nubia. One of the most important artifacts of his time, the “Israel Stela,” contains the first recorded mention of Israel.

Mummy of King Thutmose IV

The “Dream Stela,” erected by Thutmose IV between the paws of the Great Sphinx at Giza, tells of how, as a young prince, he fell asleep in the shade of this colossal figure while hunting in the desert nearby. The Sphinx appeared to him in his dreams and instructed him to clear away the sand that covered its body; in return, he would be made the next pharaoh. This suggests that he might not have been the legitimate crown prince, but that he usurped the throne in some way. Thutmose IV carried out at least one campaign to Nubia, which may have been more of a police action than anything else. He is known to have established peaceful relations with the Mitanni, Egypt’s greatest rivals to the northeast, marrying a Mitanni princess. He is credited with building a double portico in the court fronting the Fourth Pylon at Karnak; also at Karnak, he built a small chapel of Egyptian alabaster.
